A History of Covaya

What follows is the best accounting the Archive has been able to assemble. It is not complete and likely will never be.

Covayan history is, above all, a history of people arriving somewhere difficult and deciding to persevere.

— Copper City Archive, introduction

The Chronicle

The record is incomplete. The Shadow Wars destroyed what couldn't be carried. The people who know the deepest history have never written it down. What follows is assembled from text, inference, and the fragments that survived.

Present Day

The Period of Re-emergence

E.o.C. 239–present (25 years ago to now)

The present era begins not with victory but with slow, uncertain quiet. Covaya emerged from the Shadow Wars diminished — outlying territories abandoned, governance fractured, population considerably smaller. The High Chancellor in Copper City has spent the last ~10 years attempting to reassert authority over regional governors who spent over three decades operating without oversight and have largely come to prefer it.

Roads are being rebuilt. Rail lines are being extended. Trade through the Mandlewurd Pass with Mirth has resumed, though cautiously. The Ableis Isles, once Covaya's most persistent military adversary, have gone silent — no raids, no envoys, no explanation. Scholars disagree about whether this is a gift or a warning.

The stars remain gone. The southern reaches remain strange. The strangeness that was always there — in the mountains, in the ruins, in the river that flows out of places nobody maps correctly — it didn't resolve when the Shadow Wars ended. It got quiet. People don't feel good about this.

Era of the Compact

The Shadow Wars

E.o.C. 212–239 (52–25 years ago)

The Shadow Wars arrived the way a bad winter arrives: gradually, then all at once. When the Transcosmic Divide was broken, the thin places in Covaya — the Strange Mountains most of all — tore open. What came through did not have names. Shadow creatures. Things shaped wrong. Presences that arrived in dark places and did not leave.

The incursions built slowly. The south was affected first, which made the warning signs easy to dismiss — the south had always been strange. By the time creatures began appearing settled territory, Covaya understood what it was facing. The response was to contract. Outlying settlements were abandoned. Interior governors held what they could and stopped reporting what they couldn't. Copper City sealed its harbor chains at night.

Eventually the Shadowfell drifted back. The boundary closed slowly, unevenly. The creatures retreated or were destroyed. Nobody won. The rebuilding began.

The Quint split during the worst years of the Shadow Wars. The Wort stayed in the northern jungle. The Tex moved south and east into the desert. Neither tribe has offered outsiders an explanation of why.

Era of the Compact

The Compact Period

E.o.C. 1–212 (264–52 years ago)

The Covayan Compact was born of exhaustion. The largest settlements along the northern coast agreed to shared governance centered on Copper City — which had a functional harbor, a trained navy, and enough surplus copper to make cooperation worthwhile. Regional governors held local authority. The High Chancellor in Copper City handled what crossed regional lines

Most importantly, Copper City handled the Ableis Conflict — a long, grinding naval war over the archipelago north of Covaya. The fight was always incredibly well matched, and it shaped Covaya's military culture profoundly, producing a professional navy and a deep institutional wariness of the sea. When the Shadow Wars began, the Ableis Conflict simply stopped. Both sides had larger problems.

Era of Settlement

The First Exiles

~450–264 years ago

The settlers came from Mirth, via the only reliable route: the Mandlewurd Pass through the Strange Mountains. The crossing was not safe. Compasses failed. Distances misbehaved. Those who made it moved north — the southern reaches near the mountains were too strange, too hostile. They followed the coast and the river valleys, leaving small settlements as they went. Copper City began as a camp near the copper mines. The mines drew people. The harbor drew trade. The question of governance became unavoidable.

Why Covayans were exiled from Mirth is not clearly recorded. Mirth's documents have their answers but the settlers don't talk about it openly. The Quint, who encountered them early, made a considered decision to observe before acting — and what they saw in those first years shaped every relationship between settler and tribe that followed. A relationship of distance and distrust.

Age of Tribes

The Quint

Est. 500–5,000 years ago

The Quint do not describe themselves as people who arrived. They are of the land — a distinction they hold seriously and outsiders have tended to read as metaphor. Their tradition was one of careful stewardship: learning the language of a place and speaking it back accurately. They tended rather than farmed. They knew the Before People's ruins. At some point their territory contracted — from wide occupation of the continent to concentration in the northern jungle — and they have not explained why to anyone outside their tradition.

Age of Ruin

The Before People

Est. 5,000–10,000+ years ago

Before the Quint's recorded presence — or alongside its earliest roots — something else was here. Their ruins remain in the foothills near the Strange Mountains: worked stone and metal built in styles that predate any known tradition. They were not destroyed. They were abandoned. No record says why. Nature grew over them, and centuries passed, and the settlers arrived and found structures they couldn't explain and mostly chose not to investigate. The ones near the mountains in particular have a reputation for making people uncomfortable in ways they struggle to articulate. Anyone who's felt it knows the sensation with quickness.

Pre-Historical

The Long Dark

Before recorded time

Something happened before any record exists. The Quint call it the Time Without Sky — the sky went wrong, the land went quiet, and what moved in that quiet were not the things that had moved before. Whether this was the same catastrophe that struck the Atolian region, or something separate entirely, is a question scholars cannot answer. The Quint haven't said. The Before People left no record of it at all.
